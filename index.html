My path here

Eight years before I touched AI, I was already being forced into systems thinking. Munich Fabric Start trade-fair preparation compressed from four months to one and a half — across 8 editions, 2022 to 2026 — through documentation, role-handoff design, and edition-over-edition refinement. Not AI. Production volume.

AI arrived in 2024 at the edges of that pipeline: color forecasting, booth design candidate generation. Not as a replacement for the system. As an accelerator on a system that already worked.

In early 2025 I took the same toolkit to Mr Zhu Restaurant in Klagenfurt — a real-world deployment of system-thinking applied to AI content production. +550% monthly Reel views. From March 2026 onward, AIGrowth Lab as my system R&D project — five locked production frameworks codifying what works.

AI didn't teach me to think this way. Production volume did. AI arrived to a system that was ready.

Principles I come back to

The instincts behind the four cases.

The way I work is shaped as much by these principles as it is by craft. I come back to them when the work gets messy, ambiguous, or high-stakes — and they are visible, in different shapes, in every case study above.

01
Architecture before populating it.
A system has to be designed before it's filled. Whether it's a trilingual compliance matrix for defense procurement, a cross-team information architecture for textile R&D, or a five-archetype output system for restaurant content — the work that matters is upstream of the work that ships.
02
Systems first, AI second.
AI doesn't teach systems thinking. Production volume does. The strongest AI integration I have ever shipped — Munich Fabric Start prep compressed from four months to one and a half — only worked because the system existed first across editions one through five. AI joined as the accelerator at edition six, never as the substitute.
03
Anti-pattern lists beat positive guidance.
What a system won't produce is more important than what it will. Brand voice rules, deployment safeguards, AI photorealism boundaries — they all live as anti-patterns first. "Don't do X" is shorter, sharper, and more enforceable than "always do Y."
04
The hardest problem is human review.
AI generates faster than humans review. The whole production-cost-reduction story lives in correctly placing the gates, not in correctly placing the tools. I have shipped systems that worked because I held the gate. I have also shipped systems that failed at handoff because the gate wasn't designed for someone else to hold.
05
Output is the test of comprehension.
What I think I understand doesn't count until I can ship something with it. Every concept I have encountered in the last two years has had to take operational form within twenty-four hours, or it doesn't count as learned. AIGrowth Lab is that discipline applied to my own learning.

The four cases, in detail.

Case 1 — Dutch MoD Tender

4 years sustained · Same buyer, third full cycle in four years · AI Regulation Diff

Problem.

Military procurement is a zero-tolerance environment — Dutch-only documentation, exhaustive specifications, every clause mapped to compliance risk and explicit financial penalty. None of the elasticity of a normal commercial brief.

Approach.

My first reaction wasn't "this is hard" — it was that the bottleneck here isn't technical, it's the information flow. Five internal teams, three working languages, one Dutch source — so in week one I didn't touch the specifications. I built the information architecture first: a trilingual briefing plus a compliance matrix where each team saw the clauses they owned, the priorities, and the deadlines backed out for them. One voice out, one clear ownership chain running from R&D to the Italian finishing mill.

AI in 2026.

When the same tender returned, I ran the new package through an AI diff against the 2023 baseline — every changed clause, shifted penalty, tightened spec surfaced before kick-off. Decisions stayed with me; risk review and alignment time dropped sharply. AI is the amplifier, not the decision-maker.

Result.

  • 2024 delivery: on-time, zero penalties, zero breaches
  • 2026: same buyer, third full cycle in four years
  • Architecture is now Seidra's standard playbook for tender-shaped work

Case 2 — Cross-Team Information Architecture

9 years sustained · ~70% cross-team alignment time reduction

Problem.

When I took over R&D, the same fabric model often had multiple drifting versions of its technical data sheet, none in a consistent format. Production couldn't find the latest dye-finish parameters. QA might be working from an outdated spec. Commercial didn't know which version held the cost and lead time. Every new model needed multiple alignment passes before five departments were on the same page.

Approach.

This wasn't a document problem — it was an information architecture problem. Templating the layout would have tidied the surface but left "who needs what" untouched, and alignment time would barely move. So I designed three principles instead: single source of truth (one sheet per model, never multiple drifting copies), role-based readability (production / QA / commercial / logistics each find their section in the same fixed place), and extensible structure (new compliance clauses or customer demands layer in — they don't trigger a rebuild).

AI later.

AI handles cross-language versions of the technical sheet and the front-end of the customer sample workflow — I verify and lock. The architecture decides what counts as truth; AI scales how fast that truth moves.

Result.

  • Cross-team alignment time reduced by ~70% per new model
  • Core architecture stable across nine years — new compliance clauses, new customer requirements layered in without rebuilding
  • Customers like Frauenschuh and Göttmann align to the standardized sample workflow rather than negotiate it
  • Same architecture quietly underpins Case 3's two parallel pipelines

Case 3 — Munich + Gottseidank Parallel Collections

8 editions sustained · 10× volume growth · 4mo → 1.5mo trade-fair prep

Problem.

Two apparel collection pipelines running in parallel for four years, both seasonal, both built on the same weave + jacquard product lines — but with completely different decision logics underneath. Munich was self-driven: I owned the theme, the booth, the buyer conversations, the post-show conversion. Gottseidank was co-developed: the customer entered at the research stage and built the cloth with us — not picking from existing.

Approach.

Owning two pipelines at once isn't about workload — it's a discipline problem. Munich gave me high creative freedom; the discipline came from filtering trends through "can this actually convert into orders." Gottseidank gave me low creative freedom; the discipline came from translating brand DNA precisely into production-ready cloth. Same product infrastructure, two completely different commercial reasoning models. Both pipelines run on the Case 2 information architecture underneath, so neither one collapses under the other's weight.

AI from 2024.

Trend and color analysis (WGSN feeds + customer history + market data) integrated by AI, translated into commercial decisions by me. Booth visual ideation accelerated with ChatGPT and Midjourney as moodboards, never as final output. Sample-system front-end accelerated — most of the 4mo → 1.5mo reduction came from there.

Areas intentionally kept human-led: Gottseidank co-development, Munich theme calls, every buyer conversation on the floor.

Result.

  • Gottseidank: ~2,000 m/yr → 20,000+ m/yr sustained, four years of co-development
  • Munich: 8 consecutive editions, prep down from 4 months to 1.5
  • 2-person team has internalized both playbooks; I now do final review only

Munich Fabric Start, 8 editions across 4 years — moodboard, booth signage, team setup, collection wall.

Case 4 — AI Content Production System

Mr Zhu real-world deployment · AIGrowth Lab system R&D · 5 reusable production frameworks

Problem.

Two years of input on AI tools — reading, testing, threading them quietly into Seidra workflows — but studying tools doesn't automatically build a production system. Mr Zhu and AIGrowth Lab are where I committed to ship nine years of systems thinking and two years of AI input as actual output.

Approach.

Mr Zhu was the early version of the system, deployed in a live hospitality environment over three months across IG and Google Business. The first month was exploratory. The second month three things locked — a copy structure, a posting cadence, and a Claude prompt structure inside a dedicated workspace. The third month ran on the templates. AIGrowth Lab is what came after: same logic, evolved into a system R&D project. The deliverable isn't the account — it's five brand-agnostic production frameworks (codified as SKILL files): carousel format, narrative post, Reel cover scoring, each as its own production line, configurable for a different brand without rebuilding the system.

One Mr Zhu Reel concept produces a full week of channel-specific output — same brand voice, multiple formats, no manual rebuild. The five SKILL files encode this pattern as configuration: swap the brand input and the same engine produces a different brand's output.

Where AI sits.

AI runs production volume — drafts, layouts, format compliance, version diffs. The judgment layer stays human: which idea ships, where the brand voice lands, what the audience actually feels. Codifying that boundary into rules someone else can run is how individual judgment becomes a transferable system.

Result.

  • Mr Zhu: +550% Reel views over the deployment, walk-in inquiries lifted (per business owner observation)
  • AIGrowth Lab: 5 brand-agnostic, reusable production frameworks
  • Validated Reel cover formula turned from intuitive judgment into a repeatable scoring rule — usable by someone else
  • Same architecture would scale across Audi / Breitling / Puma at LOOP — configuration changes, spine doesn't

One restaurant, six channel-specific assets — Reel cover, Google Business post, student campaign, mascot illustration, recipe game video. Same brand voice, multiple formats and channels.
